A strategy to transform climate risk in Miami Beach

Miami Beach is a vulnerable, low-lying residential and tourist destination on the frontlines of climate risk. Yet the biggest challenge isn’t just water, but how the city responds to it. As flooding, extreme rainfall, and sea level rise intensify, this project explores how residents can move from awareness to action. It is grounded in transformational adaptation, using education as an empowering tool to shift the community from ignoring risk to engaging with site-based strategies that rethink how water is managed and how climate change is understood and lived.

Risk is increasing in Miami Beach

Low elevation raises exposure

Globally, sea levels are rising as the average temperature is rising. This is primarily due to thermal expansion of ocean water and melting land ice. As a low-lying coastal city, Miami is vulnerable to the effects of sea level rise (Miami.gov).

Storm risk

Miami has always been the one of the most vulnerable cities in the U.S. to hurricanes due to its subtropical climate and coastal location in the Atlantic basin. Over time, Miamians may see increased frequency/instances of Category 4 and 5 storms (NOAA AOML).

Why is adapting in preparation for climate change important?

Investing in climate adaptation can help cities reduce or avoid future losses, bring social and environmental benefits, and produce positive economic outcomes via innovation, increased productivity, and risk reduction (Miami.gov).

How risk assessment should feel

Consequentiality

By providing specific risk predictions, consequences also arise. Risk predictions shape investments, rebuilding decisions, insurance conversations, emergency staging, and public communication. Maps that predict risk need to be made with usefulness in mind, as these maps will have a large impact on future decisions within the community.

Appropriateness

The response the city of Miami Beach has should match the climate risk researchers expect. The main idea of this goal is to not overestimate or underestimate risk, but to have an adequate idea of what to expect of the future. By assessing probability, exposure, and potential impact, Miami Beach can prepare in a way that feels purposeful and useful to residents.

Meaningfulness

Risk should be accessible and understandable for all residents. Residents specifically care about how this risk impacts them at a local level. Having accessible information on specific streets and locations can help residents understand what choices are available to them before catastrophe hits.

A 3-part transformation plan

1. Preparedness:

  • Use up-to-date flood maps, climate models, and neighborhood observations to intentionally plan and understand risk more accurately.
  • Harness evolving climate science to inform real-time planning and response decisions.

2. Education + action:

  • Create immersive, hands-on experiences that help residents understand what risk means for their homes, streets, and neighborhoods.
  • Combine storytelling, real-time observation, and interactive tools so residents can safely experience future climate scenarios, build awareness, and take ownership of how they prepare.

3. Infrastructure strategies:

  • Build systems that can hold, slow, redirect, and live with water.
  • Includes water squares, green spaces, collection sites, and roof-based storage.

Preparedness

We aim to replace Miami Beach’s current reactive response system with a prepared system that integrates the most up to date climate information with a clear action plan.

1

Assess climate risk

Using updated climate modeling and regional projections, we can better quantify climate change risks in Miami Beach. Focusing on forward thinking climate forecasts instead of basing risk solely on historical data can reduce risk and exposure.

2

Define thresholds of risk

Most of Miami Beach is particularly vulnerable to multiple hazards. If a specific area has a high rainfall probability as well as elevated tide levels, flood risk is expanded beyond normal thresholds. Incorporating multiple types of risk into adaptation planning can ensure in-depth preparedness for climate change.

3

Prepare for action

Proactively create plans, inform residents, and allocate specific resources to vulnerable areas on a hyper-local neighborhood scale before climate events happen.

4

Learn and update

After each event, compare forecasts, observations, and observed flooding to ensure action aligns with anticipated risk.

Education + action

Public education should not only inform people about climate risk. It should ground people as active participants in their future, building ownership and autonomy while allowing them to safely and visually practice how to respond to risk.

Immersive climate experiences

Instead of only seeing flood maps, residents could step into future scenarios. Using AI immersive technology, people can safely experience how extreme rainfall, tides, and groundwater rise might affect their own block. These spaces could also show what transformation looks like: how water is stored, redirected and lived with.

Art, storytelling & citizen science

Public art becomes a way to reframe Miami Beach’s relationship with water. The goal is to give residents ownership over how climate resilience is understood and talked about in their own neighborhoods. Storytelling can take many forms: community murals that map past and future flood lines, photo and audio projects capturing lived experiences with high tides, or neighborhood exhibits that visualize future climate scenarios. Working with local businesses such as ‘O, Miami,’ the city could help bring creative initiatives like zipcode-based storytelling to life, building hyper-local narratives about climate and place across Miami Beach.

Residents can engage in citizen science projects, turning everyday observations into shared knowledge about future risk and transformation. Residents document experiences with water inundation in real time, contributing to a living dataset while building awareness of what’s happening on the ground. The aim is to connect what people see on the ground to action-creating a two-way exchange where resident observations directly inform city understanding and response.

Climate block parties + resource hubs

Shift from a traditional resource fair to something that feels like Miami: a neighborhood block party with local food vendors and music. Streets or parks become spaces where residents can explore flood barriers, talk with city staff, learn about insurance and elevation options, and see live demonstrations of adaptation strategies. Residents can learn about how to protect their home, what to do before a storm, and what current adaptation strategies are in place.

Infrastructure strategies

By treating water as something to work with, and not against, we can integrate flexible systems that can store, manage, and reduce flood risk over time

Water squares

A water square in Miami Beach could act as an everyday public space, such as a plaza or event area when it is dry, but temporarily store stormwater during intense rainfall events. In a city where short, high-intensity storms can overwhelm drainage systems, these spaces provide visible, local storage that reduces street flooding while keeping public space active and usable. A pilot site, such as Collins Park, could demonstrate how Miami Beach can serve as the first multi-functional public space designed to operate in both dry and wet conditions.

Green spaces

Parks in Miami Beach already play a role in flood management, and this approach can be expanded. Features like bioswales, rain gardens, and permeable surfaces help slow and store water before it reaches streets and storm drains. Projects like Bayshore Park and Canopy Park already show how green spaces can double as infrastructure, reducing flood risk while creating Beautifying the city and creating space for the community to gather.

Blue roofs

Rooftops can be designed to actively manage water instead of immediately sending it into the drainage system. By retrofitting buildings with blue roof systems, rainwater can be temporarily held during intense storms and released slowly after peak rainfall has passed, reducing the surge that overwhelms streets and storm drains. Stored water can also be reused for building cooling or plumbing, helping reduce deman on water resources. When applied across many buildings, blue roofs create a distributed system that delays and spreads out runoff over time, using existing space to support Miami Beach’s drainage system in a practical and scalable way.

“The future will not be like the past, and our systems must be designed for what’s coming, not what we’ve already seen.”

Transformational adaptation means Miami Beach plans for events before they happen, using forecasts and risk signals to prepare spaces for water, not just reacting after flooding occurs. This strategy connects three pieces: better risk assessment, public education, and infrastructure that makes room for water.
